What is AQI?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a comprehensive system used by environmental agencies to translate complex chemical data into a single, understandable safety score.

Our dashboard calculates this index by evaluating ground-level concentrations of PM2.5, PM10, and NO2 emissions across selected urban nodes.

Our Project Aims

  • 01/ Isolate and map micro-scale pollution hotspots that often go undetected by generalized regional sensors.
  • 02/ Empower urban residents with precise biometric feedback to mitigate long-term respiratory exposure.
  • 03/ Standardize environmental data visualization through a responsive, high-fidelity monitoring interface.

Reference: Air Quality Scale

AQI RANGE BIOLOGICAL IMPACT SAFETY PROTOCOL
0 - 50
Stable		 Atmospheric purity is high. Baseline data suggests negligible physiological impact on standard respiratory functions. Unrestricted outdoor activity.
51 - 100
Acceptable		 Pollutant levels are manageable; however, individuals with hypersensitivity may notice early-onset irritation. Sensitive groups should evaluate exertion levels during prolonged exposure periods.
101 - 150
Cautionary		 Elevated concentrations likely to affect vulnerable demographics. Minimal threat to the general public. Children and elderly inhabitants should minimize intensive physical exertion in outdoor environments.
151 - 200
Unhealthy		 Observable respiratory aggravation across wider populations. Significant stress for those with pre-existing conditions. Restrict prolonged outdoor exposure. Implement the use of high-grade protective filtration (N95+).
201 - 300
Severe		 Systemic health risks detected. High probability of acute respiratory incidents across all age groups. Strict avoidance of all outdoor exertion. Transition to sealed environments with mechanical air purification.
300+
Hazardous		 Emergency toxicological status. Immediate risk of permanent pulmonary damage for all urban inhabitants. Cease all outdoor activity. Full deployment of indoor air scrubbers and emergency safety measures.
